Frequently asked questions


On April 18, 2009, the Harris County Toll Road Authority (HCTRA) opened the Katy Managed Lanes for full operation. A new type of roadway for the Houston region, these lanes combine two familiar transportation options—High Occupancy Vehicle (HOV) lanes and toll roads—to more flexibly meet the needs of a wide range of commuters, from mass transit and HOV riders to Single Occupancy Vehicles.

The Katy Managed Lanes include two lanes in each direction between State Highway 6 and Interstate Highway 610 West that replace the single, reversible HOV lane. The lanes offer more reliable travel times for METRO buses and HOV riders, while making any unused capacity available to drivers who pay a toll for the potential time savings.

This ambitious project was a team effort of Harris County, the Texas Department of Transportation (TxDOT), the Federal Highway Administration and METRO. By partnering with the Texas Department of Transportation to leverage its federal funding, HCTRA provided the resources necessary to fast-track the project, cut construction time in half and offer a new commuting option for our region’s drivers. HCTRA is charged with operating and maintaining the managed lanes. Harris County Constables provide strict enforcement of roadway rules.


What are Managed Lanes?

Managed lanes serve the needs of HOV, mass transit and tollway users in a single roadway. Mass transit and HOV riders use the lanes free of charge during HOV hours and any unused capacity is made available to single drivers who pay a toll. Roadway operations are set to keep traffic moving so that all users can benefit from relatively predictable travel times. A flexible traffic-management tool, managed lanes are designed to ease congestion during peak travel periods for all commuters.
What's the difference between HOV Lanes and Managed Lanes?

Managed lanes are the next-generation HOV lanes. Traditionally, HOV lanes are available only to those vehicles having the required number of passengers (HOV2+ or HOV3+), which means that valuable roadway capacity often goes unused. Managed lanes maximize this unused capacity by making the roadway available to all vehicles. The needs of mass transit and HOV users remain prioritized by setting toll rates for Single Occupancy Vehicles (SOV) to maintain the flow of traffic during peak hours. During non-peak hours, managed lanes operate like a regular tollway where all drivers (except mass transit) pay a toll to use the road.
What's the difference between a Managed Lane and a Toll Road?

On a toll road, all vehicles pay a toll, which is determined by the number of axles. On the Katy Managed Lanes, single drivers pay a toll that varies depending on demand, while mass-transit, motorcycle and HOV2+ users ride free during HOV hours. All other times, the managed lanes function as a regular tollway.
What is dynamic tolling and how will it work?

Dynamic tolling, also called “value pricing,” means that the toll rate changes based on variables like the amount of traffic on the roadway and the time of day.

The Katy Managed Lanes toll rates vary by time of day, and follow a schedule that reflects traffic volumes. Tolling does not apply to HOV users during HOV hours.

In the future, a system may be implemented using real-time technology to monitor activity on the managed lanes, with tolls increasing for single drivers as traffic increases in the lanes.
Where does the money go that is collected on the Managed Lanes?

The tolls collected from the Katy Managed Lanes—and all HCTRA-managed toll roads—go right back into Harris County and help manage and maintain our system of roadways, as well as help fund the upkeep of other county roads.
Aren't these lanes affordable only to those who can pay the premium tolls?

Managed lanes are designed to help all motorists on the highway. Motorists who need to be somewhere at a certain time have the option to pay a toll to access the managed lanes, which in turn helps ease congestion in the main lanes. Additionally, because HOV users ride free during peak hours, managed lanes encourage people to share rides to avoid congestion, reducing the number of vehicles on the road.

A report from a Los Angeles-based think tank concluded that people of all income levels use managed lanes when they are short of time. For example, a parent who’s running late to pick up a child at daycare will likely spend $2 or $3 on tolls rather than $20 on late fees.

Most drivers will not use the lanes every day. People who choose to stay in the regular lanes pay in minutes instead of money, the report says.

Why doesn't HCTRA just stick to fixed pricing? It's what Houston drivers already understand.

HCTRA is committed to maintaining the efficiency of the lanes for their originally intended users—METRO buses, carpoolers and emergency vehicles. To achieve this level of service, tolls must be higher during peak travel hours to keep traffic moving and effectively manage use.
Who is expected to use the Katy Managed Lanes?

The use of the Katy Managed Lanes is optional to all motorists traveling along the Katy Freeway, including current HOV users. Some motorists may choose to be regular users and others may only use them when they feel the time savings is offset by the cost of paying a toll. Carpoolers and vanpoolers will always have the option to use the Katy Managed Lanes during peak times at no charge.
When should motorists use the Katy Managed Lanes versus the general-purpose lanes?

Commuters have the choice of using the type of lane that best fits their needs. Both types are available all day; however, the managed lanes are generally more appropriate for longer commutes than shorter trips.
How will the Katy Managed Lanes ease congestion?

The Katy Managed Lanes represent one response to help ease the impact of congestion by offering an alternative to spending valuable time in traffic. Drivers have the option to use the Katy Managed Lanes when time is most valuable—which helps ease congestion in the main lanes.
What differentiates the Katy Managed Lanes from the general-purpose lanes?

The managed lanes are physically separated from the general-purpose lanes by a series of flexible “candlestick” barriers. Clearly posted overhead signage designates the lane that drivers should use to enter and exit the facility.
Who can use the Katy Managed Lanes?

The Katy Managed Lanes are open to all vehicles but rules of use depend on the type of vehicle and number of occupants. METRO buses and school buses ride free 24 hours a day, seven days a week. Vehicles with two or more occupants can ride free during HOV hours but must pay a toll to use the roadway at all other times. Single drivers who want the benefit of more predictable travel times can only ride the managed lanes by paying a toll.
Will motorcycles be allowed to use the Katy Managed Lanes?

Yes. Motorcycles qualify as HOV 2+ on the Katy Managed Lanes and may use the facility free of charge during HOV hours. Note: Motorcycles must pay a toll on all other Harris County Toll Road Authority facilities, as well as on the managed lanes during non-HOV hours.
Where are the entry and exit points for the Katy Managed Lanes?

There are three outbound and four inbound entrances and exits between SH 6 and IH 610. A detailed map of the roadway is available here.
What are the HOV hours for the Katy Managed Lanes?

5 am to 11 am and 2 pm to 8 pm, Monday through Friday, both eastbound and westbound.
Do I have to have an EZ TAG to use the Katy Managed Lanes?

You can only use the managed lanes as a toll customer if you have a traditional EZ TAG or an EZ TAG Express account, or another interoperable Texas toll tag, provided your account is in good standing and the vehicle’s license plate is registered with your tag; cash is not accepted on this roadway. At this time, HOV users do not need an EZ TAG or TxTag to use the managed lanes during HOV hours.
Do I need to register to use the Managed Lanes?

Not at this time, although registration may be required in the future.
How many people do I have to have in a vehicle to qualify for HOV?

Two or more.
How do I identify myself as an HOV or toll user when I'm riding the Managed Lanes?

HOV users should drive through the tolling area in the left-hand lane, while toll-paying users should use the right-hand lane to declare their status during HOV hours. Follow the clearly posted signs as you approach a tolling area and make any necessary lane changes as far in advance as possible.
How will HOV 2+, safety and traffic control measures be enforced?

HCTRA will maintain a strong law enforcement presence along the managed lanes to ensure drivers are following the HOV 2+ rule. Citations will be given to motorists who violate the toll and HOV rules, as well as roadway safety and traffic control laws.
What did it cost to build the new Managed Lanes?

HCTRA’s investment in the Katy Freeway reconstruction project was $250 million. These funds were generated from toll collections and not from tax revenue.
Do other cities have managed lanes on freeways?

Yes, San Diego, Miami, Minneapolis, Salt Lake City and Denver already use managed lanes. The Katy Managed Lanes are the first of their kind in Texas.
Is Harris County considering extending HOV to their existing Toll Road system?

Not at this time. The County’s existing toll road system was not designed to incorporate HOV traffic. The Katy Managed Lanes were made possible because of the reconstruction of the Katy Freeway undertaken by the Texas Department of Transportation.

Why didn't you just add two more general-purpose lanes instead of having Managed Lanes?

Limited land availability, scarce funds and social and environmental issues make building larger and larger freeways impractical. The combination of these factors is forcing transportation planners and engineers to explore new ways to leverage technology to more effectively operate the current transportation network, such as incorporating managed lane facilities into existing roadways.
Who should I call if there's an accident or if I break down on the Managed Lanes?

HCTRA provides roadside assistance through our Incident Response Team. This dedicated team regularly patrols the roadway and can be reached by calling 713.222.7328. The number is listed on the back of your EZ TAG.
